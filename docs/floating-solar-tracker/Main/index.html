Overview​

Executive Summary​

The overall objective of this project was to provide campers and hikers with an appropriate product that delivers off grid power while outdoors. More specifically, the primary objective was to create an IoT-compatible floating solar panel unit, which automatically tracked (aligned) its panel to the sun. The unit must do this efficiently (absorb the max amount of power, and use the least amount of power) while being durable, lightweight, environmentally friendly, and cost effective. The methodology used in developing the solution (the product) commenced with conceptualizing. Here, stakeholder needs (campers, environmental organizations, etc) were translated to product requirements. After deriving the floating solar panel’s required functions, and the ways in which they can be achieved (different sun tracking mechanisms, floatation devices, etc), design concepts were sketched. These concepts were then evaluated based on camper (and other) needs. The optimal concept was found to be an amalgamation of two separate design concepts. This solution was found to be lightweight, durable, easy to assemble, and cheap to fabricate. The methodology moved on to technical analysis, determining critical component sizes (such as floatation device size needed, battery size needed, etc). This phase also determined other performance metrics to be met, such as the required torque capacity of motors, and motion range of the chosen tracking mechanism. This phase was followed by prototype fabrication and testing. This methodology resulted in the prototype meeting all of the main user requirements. The prototype was able to float on water using PET soda bottles, tracked the sun with its two axis mechanism (thus efficiently collecting solar power), and was IoT compatible. Additionally, it fulfils the secondary goals by being. Its portability, durability, and environmental sustainability is thanks to its sturdy construction consisting of aluminum, 3D printed PLA, and PET plastic bottles. Its weather resistance is due to the design’s inclusion of O-rings and gaskets. Its user-friendly design is attributed to its simple construction, and inclusion of minimalist components like buckles. This allows for easy disassembly and reassembly during transportation.The prototype was not without its imperfections. Feedback (outside of the team) noted that the access to enclosed electronics could have been made easier, and the PET bottles are susceptible to some long-term environmental degradation.

Future fixes: Using snap-fit modular joints, strengthening holder stress spots, and more thoroughly verifying waterproofing with more gaskets. A more durable, sunlight resistant floatation device would be used in the future.

Despite this, the prototype floating solar panel unit shows promise as a concept. The shortcomings mentioned above can be feasibly addressed with minor design changes, while maintaining and enhancing the prototype’s strengths. Overall positive results of the prototype proved that the team’s efforts have met user requirements. The team’s collaboration (among members), application of mechanical engineering principles, and application of product design principles, resulted in a product that can supply campers with grid power while being outdoors.

Objectives​

Primary objective​

The primary objective is to design and create an Ardunino controlled, IoT based sun tracking solar panel that can autonomously track the sun and maximize solar energy absorption. The goal is to provide a reliable off grid power source for outdoor enthusiasts such as campers and hikers. The system will enhance efficiency, portability and ease of use while ensuring power generation in remote areas.

Secondary Objectives​

The secondary objectives focus on enhancing the efficiency, usability, and sustainability. The first major secondary objective is energy efficiency optimization. The system should use dual axis and high efficiency solar panels to track and absorb energy from the sun to meet the user's needs. Furthermore, the system must be portable and durable to ensure practicality in harsh outdoor environments. This can be accomplished by using a lightweight and weather resistant material. Moreover, the system must use an IoT based remote monitoring. The system should provide real time data and remote control through Arduino IoT cloud and integrated sensors. Lastly, the system must be cost effective and sustainable. The design must maintain affordability within a 200$ budget as regular consumers for recreational use have a limited expense.

Through embodied development, the design was greatly improved by taking into account test findings, materials accessibility issues, assembly difficulties, and input from TA evaluations.

Project Reflection and Evaluation​

Conceptual Design Phase​
  1. Mistakes in initial requirements/objectives: As pool noodles are readily available and have a reputation for being buoyant, we initially chose them for flotation. However, their limited structural stiffness and bulkiness were not fully taken into consideration. Our portability and compactness goals were harmed by this error, necessitating a major revision later on.
  2. Final design alignment with original objectives:  Our final design mostly matched the initial goals. Dual-axis solar tracking, portability, affordability, and adaptability were all effectively preserved. The switch to 1L plastic bottles improved portability and buoyancy, better meeting the demands of hikers and campers.
  3. Unmet requirements: The majority of the design specifications were fulfilled. The additional buckles and mounting systems, which were necessary for structural security and versatility, caused a little variation in the overall dimensions. However, these have no discernible impact on weight or overall portability.
  4. Similarity of final to selected conceptual design: Although it was refined, the final design conceptually stayed close to Design 1. In addition to optimising electronic housing (Main body) dimensions and incorporating buckles for easy assembly and transportation, the floating medium was changed from pool noodles to 1L bottles. Weight distribution and component connectivity were improved by these modifications.
  5. Viability of other conceptual designs:Designs 2 through 4 were promising, but their complexity (propellers, inflatables, etc.) went against our objectives for durability and use. The final design that was selected provided the best possible balance between usability, performance, and simplicity.
Embodiment Design Phase​
  1. Critical components to analyze: The performance of the servo motor and the flotation mechanism were the most important elements to examine. For dependable operation and to avoid failure modes like sinking or motor stalling, it was crucial to guarantee the buoyancy and torque capacity.
  2. Underperforming parts: The original flotation holders were too heavy, however no parts completely failed. By using less material and having less mass overall, their redesign into lighter and smaller versions enhanced performance.
  3. Design calculation refinements: To verify our design, we employed sensitivity analyses (such as torque under wind load and buoyancy calculations). Dynamic models in wind and wave conditions could be used in future developments to fine-tune bottle size and placement.
  4. Overlooked aspects/calculations: At first, we failed to consider the impact of bracket location on the centre of buoyancy versus centre of gravity. We greatly increased stability by moving the bottle holders from the bottom to the sides in response to criticism. A design calculation that was neglected was the fixed resistor sizing calculation needed for the LDR (Light Dependent Resistor). This calculation is important because the Arduino MKR WiFi 1010 can be potentially damaged from too much voltage applied to its input terminals from the LDR input power. Performing a voltage divider calculation to size the required resistor would have ensured that there would be no risk for input terminal damage during assembly and testing. Additionally, this sizing calculation would have quantified a fixed resistance needed that would allow the LDR input signals to vary enough with light exposure (be sensitive enough). 
Detail Design, Prototyping, and Testing​
  1. Modifications due to fabrication experience: Yes, we discovered the original pool noodle holders were too big after we started working with the materials. By making the bottle holders slimmer and lighter, we were able to use less material and less weight. Additionally, we added buckles to make it easier for the holders to latch onto the main housing, increasing its portability and adaptability.
  2. Testing challenges: During indoor tests, we had trouble mimicking real-world conditions (such as the intensity of sunlight and sea waves). We attempted to simulate solar movement using flashlights, however this was not quite accurate. It took longer to assess stability in real-world water conditions.
  3. Prototype performance vs. expectations: The prototype largely operated as anticipated, although we noticed some minor tilting due to an unequal weight distribution. Rearranging the bottle holder and distributing the mass evenly on the aluminium sheet fixed this.
  4.  Impact of more pre-build modeling: It would have been great if we  had a more thorough pre-build simulation (such as CFD for water flow or structural stress testing) that would have improved our ability to forecast performance in the actual world. In particular, fewer changes would be required if the structural stress on bottle holders and their mounting locations were modelled.
  5. Shortcomings and future improvements: Some shortcomings include:
    • Holder durability: Despite being lightweight, additional material testing (for example, under repeated mechanical stress) may increase longevity.
    • Electronics access: It's a little laborious to open the electronics case; a quick-release mechanism would make it easier to use.
    • Floatation device durability: The final floatation devices selected (plastic soda bottles) are susceptible to puncturing from sharp environmental objects (such as rocks). This puncturing can lead to water flooding in the bottles. Additionally, the plastic soda bottles are susceptible to degradation through sunlight. These considerations were overlooked.
    • Future fixes: Using snap-fit modular joints, strengthening holder stress spots, and more thoroughly verifying waterproofing with more gaskets. A more durable, sunlight resistant floatation device would be used in the future.
